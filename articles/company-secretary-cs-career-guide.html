Introduction: The Silent Guardian at the Heart of Every Corporation

Picture the boardroom of a major Indian corporation. The directors are gathered. A significant decision is on the table — a merger, a major policy change, a new capital raise. The lawyers are present. The financial advisors are present. And seated at that table, quietly indispensable, is a professional whose role is unlike any other in the room: the Company Secretary. They are the person who ensured that every director was properly appointed. They are the one who guarantees this meeting is legally valid. They are the guardian of the company's compliance record, the custodian of its governance standards, and the advisor whose counsel protects the organization from the legal landmines that lie hidden in every significant corporate decision.

In the complex, regulation-dense, fast-moving world of modern Indian business, the Company Secretary — commonly known as a CS — occupies a position of extraordinary importance and extraordinary responsibility. They are simultaneously a legal expert, a compliance officer, a corporate governance authority, a strategic advisor to the Board of Directors, and the human bridge between a company and the regulatory ecosystem that governs its existence. Without a Company Secretary, a company navigates corporate law essentially blindfolded — exposed to risks that it does not see until they materialize as crises, penalties, or reputational damage that can take years to repair.

This is precisely why, under the Companies Act of India, every company of a certain size is legally required to appoint a qualified Company Secretary. This is not a suggestion. It is a statutory obligation. And it means that the demand for qualified CS professionals in India is not driven by market trends or corporate fashion — it is driven by law itself. The career opportunity is structural, enduring, and growing steadily as India's corporate sector expands, as regulatory frameworks become more complex, and as organizations increasingly recognize that good corporate governance is not just a legal requirement but a genuine competitive advantage.

If you have a mind that is drawn to law, to governance, to the intersection of business and regulation, and to the kind of meticulous, detail-oriented professional work that carries real organizational consequence — then a career as a Company Secretary may be the most fitting and most rewarding professional path available to you. This comprehensive guide will walk you through every aspect of the journey: what a CS truly does, the structure of the prestigious ICSI course that qualifies you for the profession, the examinations you must pass, the training you must complete, the skills you must develop, the salary you can expect, and the remarkable range of roles and organizations that will be open to you when you earn those two transformative letters after your name.

What Is a Company Secretary?

A Company Secretary is a senior professional who serves as the principal compliance officer and corporate governance authority within an organization. Also known by the titles of Corporate Secretary and Secretary to the Board, the CS is the individual ultimately responsible for ensuring that a company's legal activities are conducted correctly, that its regulatory filings are complete and timely, that its tax returns are properly prepared, that its records are accurately maintained, and that its Board of Directors receives the expert guidance they need to make decisions that are not just commercially sound but legally defensible and ethically appropriate.

But defining a CS purely in terms of compliance and paperwork is, again, a profound understatement of the role's true scope and significance. A great Company Secretary is one of the most strategically important professionals in a company's leadership ecosystem. They are the person who advises the Board on the legal implications of major corporate decisions before those decisions are made — not after. They are the architect of the company's governance framework — the policies, procedures, and structures that determine how power is exercised, how accountability is maintained, and how the company protects the interests of all its stakeholders, from shareholders and employees to creditors and the public. And they are the expert navigator who guides the company through the increasingly dense and increasingly demanding regulatory environment that governs corporate activity in India.

The specific responsibilities that define the CS role are both wide-ranging and deeply consequential. They include managing all legal activities of the company, ensuring compliance with the Companies Act and all other applicable legislation, completing and filing company tax returns, maintaining the company's statutory registers and records, organizing and minuting Board meetings and Annual General Meetings, advising the Board of Directors on corporate governance best practices, managing relationships with regulatory bodies including the Ministry of Corporate Affairs and SEBI, overseeing share transfers and shareholder communications, and — in many organizations — serving as a key advisor on major transactions including mergers, acquisitions, joint ventures, and capital market activities. Each of these responsibilities, done well, directly protects the company and its stakeholders. Done inadequately, the consequences — regulatory penalties, legal challenges, reputational damage — can be severe.

Why Choose Company Secretaryship? The Case for This Remarkable Career

In a professional landscape crowded with options, why should you invest the years of rigorous preparation that the CS qualification demands? The answer lies in the unique combination of career attributes that the CS designation offers — attributes that are genuinely difficult to find in any other single professional qualification.

Statutory Demand — The Law Itself Creates Your Job Market

The most fundamental advantage of the CS qualification is something that no other professional designation in India can quite match: the demand for your services is not subject to market cycles or corporate budget decisions in the way that most professional roles are. Under the Companies Act, 2013, every company with a paid-up share capital of ₹10 crore or more is legally required to appoint a whole-time Company Secretary. Every listed company must have one. The consequence is a baseline demand for CS professionals that is statutory, predictable, and growing as more companies cross the threshold that triggers the mandatory appointment requirement. Your career prospects are, in a very literal sense, backed by an Act of Parliament.

A Profession at the Intersection of Law, Finance, and Strategy

The CS qualification is unusual among professional certifications in the extraordinary breadth of knowledge it develops. A qualified CS is not just a legal expert — they are also deeply knowledgeable about corporate finance, taxation, financial management, capital markets, and business strategy. This breadth makes the CS uniquely valuable as a senior advisor who can contribute meaningfully to discussions that span the full complexity of a corporate organization's strategic and operational life. The CS who can speak fluently about both the legal implications of a proposed transaction and its financial structuring is a fundamentally more valuable advisor than any specialist who can only address one dimension.

Unmatched Access to the Boardroom

There are very few professional roles that give you direct, sustained access to the highest levels of an organization's leadership from relatively early in your career. The CS is one of them. Because the Company Secretary serves as the Secretary to the Board of Directors, you are present at — and a contributor to — the most consequential discussions that take place within a company. You advise the Chairman and Managing Director. You interact regularly with Non-Executive Directors and Independent Directors. You are present when major strategic decisions are made, and your voice — on governance, on legal compliance, on regulatory risk — is genuinely listened to and genuinely influential. That access to organizational leadership, and the professional development that comes with it, is one of the most powerful career accelerants available in Indian business.

The Institute of Company Secretaries of India (ICSI)

The pathway to becoming a qualified Company Secretary in India runs, without exception, through a single institution: the Institute of Company Secretaries of India, universally known as ICSI. Established under an Act of Parliament, ICSI is the sole recognized professional body authorized to develop and regulate the Company Secretaries profession in India. It is ICSI that sets the curriculum, conducts the examinations, maintains the register of qualified CS professionals, and ensures the continuing professional development of its members throughout their careers.

ICSI's reach is extraordinary — it provides training and education to hundreds of thousands of candidates across India through a network of regional and chapter offices, and it conducts the ICSI CS examination, which is a national-level examination and one of the most rigorous professional qualification assessments in India's corporate and legal education landscape. The course itself is structured as a distance learning program — ICSI provides comprehensive course materials at the time of registration, and candidates study through self-directed learning supplemented by optional oral coaching classes that are available at ICSI's network of authorized coaching centres across the country.

This distance learning model is one of the CS qualification's most practically appealing features. Unlike full-time degree programs that require you to be physically present in a classroom for fixed hours every day, the CS course can be pursued alongside other studies or, for working professionals, alongside employment. The flexibility this provides — the ability to progress toward a prestigious professional qualification without sacrificing the financial income or the work experience that comes from being employed — makes the CS qualification accessible to a remarkably wide range of aspirants at different life stages and in different personal circumstances.

The CS Course Structure — Three Levels to Professional Qualification

To achieve the CS qualification and the right to practice Company Secretaryship at the professional level, you must successfully complete three distinct levels of training and examination set by ICSI. Each level builds on the last, deepening your knowledge and your professional capabilities in a carefully structured progression from foundational concepts to advanced professional practice.

Level 1: CS Executive Entrance Test (CSEET) — Your Gateway to the Profession

The first level of the CS qualification journey is the CS Executive Entrance Test — commonly known as the CSEET. This examination was introduced as a replacement for the earlier CS Foundation Programme, which was discontinued when ICSI undertook a significant restructuring of the CS course. The CSEET is now a compulsory qualifying test that every candidate must pass before they can register for the Executive Programme — the second level of the CS course. There are no exceptions to this requirement for most candidates, making the CSEET the essential gateway through which every new CS aspirant must pass.

To be eligible to sit the CSEET, you need to have passed Class 12 (10+2) from a recognized board. There is one important exception to the CSEET requirement: candidates who have passed the CS Foundation Programme under the older course structure, or who have passed the Final Course of the Institute of Chartered Accountants of India (ICAI), or who have passed the Final Course of the Institute of Cost Accountants of India (ICMAI), are exempted from the CSEET and may register directly for the Executive Programme. For everyone else, the CSEET is the mandatory starting point.

The CSEET is a Computer Based Test (CBT) conducted online, and it is a genuinely modern and technologically sophisticated examination. It includes multiple choice questions, audio clips, video clips, and descriptive questions — a format that tests not just textbook knowledge but also comprehension, communication, and the ability to process and respond to information presented in different media. The total duration of the examination is 135 minutes, including the time allocated for reading the questions. There is no negative marking — a genuinely candidate-friendly feature that encourages confident, thoughtful attempts rather than risk-averse omissions.

The subjects covered in the CSEET are Business Communication, Legal Aptitude and Logical Reasoning, Economic and Business Environment, Current Affairs, and Presentation and Communication Skills. To pass the CSEET, a candidate must secure a minimum of 40% marks in each individual subject and an aggregate of 50% marks overall. The examination fee is ₹1,000, with concessions available for SC/ST candidates and candidates with physical disabilities. The CSEET result, once declared, remains valid for eight years — giving candidates a generous window within which to register for and complete the Executive Programme before their CSEET qualification expires.

The CSEET is scheduled to be held four times a year — in the months of May, July, November, and January — giving candidates multiple opportunities to attempt the examination each year and minimizing the waiting time between attempts if additional preparation is needed. The examination has been conducted in remote proctored mode, allowing candidates to sit the test from their homes rather than traveling to examination centres — a feature that dramatically increases accessibility for candidates in smaller cities and towns.

CSEET Exemption — Who Qualifies?

You do NOT need to sit the CSEET if you have: (1) passed the CS Foundation Programme under the old ICSI course structure, (2) passed the Final Course of ICAI (Chartered Accountants), or (3) passed the Final Course of ICMAI (Cost Accountants). If any of these apply to you, you may register directly for the CS Executive Programme — a significant time-saving advantage for professionals who have already completed comparable professional qualifications.

Level 2: CS Executive Programme — Building Your Professional Knowledge Base

The CS Executive Programme is the second and substantively the most demanding level of the CS qualification journey. It is at this level that you begin to build the deep, comprehensive knowledge of corporate law, financial management, taxation, and governance that will define you as a professional CS. The Executive Programme is not a beginner-level course — it expects a genuine intellectual engagement with complex legal and financial concepts, and it demands the kind of sustained, disciplined study that builds real professional competence rather than surface-level familiarity.

To register for the Executive Programme, a candidate must have passed the CSEET (or be eligible for direct entry as described above), and must be at least 17 years of age at the time of application. Registration for the Executive Programme is open twice a year — in June and in December — giving candidates two annual windows to enter the programme. Once registered, the registration remains valid for five years, within which the candidate must complete both the Executive Programme and the subsequent Professional Programme. If a candidate is unable to complete both programmes within this five-year window, they must apply for a registration extension.

The registration fee for the Executive Programme is ₹2,000 for all candidates. The total programme fee varies based on the route through which the candidate has qualified: candidates who have passed the CSEET or the old CS Foundation Programme pay a total fee of ₹10,600, while candidates who have passed the Final Course of ICAI or ICMAI pay a total fee of ₹15,600. These fees reflect the different starting points of different candidate groups but lead to the same rigorous programme of study and the same comprehensive examinations.

Upon passing the Executive Programme examinations, candidates are required to complete two additional components before they can progress to the Professional Programme. The first is a one-month Executive Development Programme (EDP) focused on soft skills and IT skills — recognizing that technical legal and financial knowledge must be complemented by the communication, presentation, and technology capabilities that modern CS professionals need. The second, and arguably more important, is 21 months of practical training in an industry or organization under the supervision of a qualified CS. This practical training component is not a formality — it is a genuine, substantive immersion in the realities of corporate practice that transforms theoretical knowledge into professional capability.

Level 3: CS Professional Programme — The Summit of the Qualification

The CS Professional Programme is the final and most advanced level of the CS qualification — the summit of the journey that began with the CSEET. It is at this level that the CS aspirant develops the advanced expertise in governance, compliance, corporate restructuring, capital markets, and strategic advisory that distinguishes a fully qualified Company Secretary from a corporate generalist with legal knowledge. The Professional Programme delves into the most complex and consequential aspects of corporate law and governance practice, preparing candidates not just to comply with regulatory requirements but to advise on them, to challenge them intelligently, and to help organizations build governance frameworks that go beyond mere compliance to genuine excellence.

To register for the Professional Programme, a candidate must have successfully completed the CS Executive Programme. Registration follows the same twice-yearly schedule as the Executive Programme — in June and December — and the total fee for the Professional Programme is ₹13,000. Like the Executive Programme, the Professional Programme registration remains valid for five years from the date of registration.

Passing the Professional Programme examinations, combined with the completion of all required training components, earns the candidate the CS designation and full membership of ICSI — the formal qualification that opens the door to professional practice as a Company Secretary. It is a qualification earned through years of rigorous study, demanding examination, and genuine professional training, and it carries with it the weight and the respect that such a journey deserves.

Essential Skills Every Company Secretary Must Master

Academic qualifications and examination passes are the foundation — but the Company Secretary who truly excels in the role, who becomes genuinely indispensable to the organizations they serve, is one who combines that foundation with a specific constellation of professional skills that no examination can fully develop. Here is an honest and comprehensive account of the skills that define an outstanding CS.

Deep Knowledge of Company Law — The Absolute Foundation

The Companies Act, 2013, and the dense ecosystem of rules, regulations, notifications, and circulars that flow from it, is the primary legal framework within which every Company Secretary operates. A CS who does not know Company Law with genuine depth and precision is a CS who cannot do their job. This knowledge must go far beyond a surface-level familiarity with the Act's provisions — it must extend to a thorough understanding of how those provisions are interpreted in practice, how they interact with other legal frameworks, how they have been applied in case law, and how they are likely to evolve as the regulatory environment continues to develop. This is not knowledge you acquire once and consider settled — it demands continuous updating, continuous engagement with new developments, and the intellectual habit of treating the law as a living, evolving framework rather than a static text.

Strong Communication Skills — Written, Spoken, and Presentational

The Company Secretary is, at their core, a communicator — someone who translates the complexity of legal and regulatory requirements into clear, precise, actionable guidance for non-specialist audiences. The minutes of a Board meeting must be a model of clarity and precision. The legal opinion provided to the Board on a proposed transaction must be comprehensive, accessible, and unambiguous. The communication to shareholders about a corporate action must be accurate, timely, and written in language that shareholders can genuinely understand. Strong written communication — the ability to produce documents that are both legally precise and humanly readable — is therefore one of the CS's most important professional skills. But spoken communication matters equally: the CS who can present a complex governance issue to a Board clearly and confidently, who can explain a legal risk in terms that resonate with businesspeople rather than lawyers, is far more effective than one whose expertise is trapped behind inaccessible technical language.

Time Management and Multitasking — The Operational Reality of the Role

The corporate calendar imposes a relentless rhythm on the Company Secretary's working life. Annual General Meetings, Board meetings, quarterly filings, tax deadlines, regulatory submissions — all of these have fixed dates, and the consequences of missing them range from financial penalties to regulatory action to personal legal liability for the CS and the company's directors. Managing multiple simultaneous deadlines, prioritizing effectively when several urgent matters compete for attention simultaneously, and maintaining the quality of work under pressure are not optional extras for a Company Secretary — they are daily professional requirements that must be met consistently and reliably, year after year.

Proficiency in English — Written and Spoken

The CS profession in India operates overwhelmingly in English. Company law, regulatory filings, Board communications, shareholder documents, legal opinions, and the vast majority of corporate governance documentation are in English. A CS whose English is inadequate — whose written English is imprecise or whose spoken English lacks confidence — is limited in the roles they can take on and the impact they can have. Investing in the development of genuine English language proficiency — both written and spoken — is not a cosmetic career investment. It is a fundamental professional requirement that will directly determine the ceiling of your impact and your career progression.

Corporate Awareness and Strategic Thinking

The most valuable Company Secretaries are those who understand the business they serve — who are not just legal technicians processing compliance tasks but genuine business thinkers who understand the strategic context of the governance work they do. Corporate awareness means understanding the company's business model, its competitive environment, its financial position, and the strategic priorities of its leadership — and bringing that understanding to bear on the governance and compliance advice you provide. A CS who can say "this proposed transaction raises governance concerns, and here's how we might structure it differently to address them while still achieving the commercial objectives" is far more valuable than one who can only say "this proposed transaction raises governance concerns."

How to Become a Company Secretary — Your Complete Roadmap

The path to becoming a qualified Company Secretary is structured, well-defined, and genuinely achievable for those who approach it with the right combination of preparation, persistence, and professional seriousness. Here is the complete roadmap.

Step 1: Complete Your Class 12 and Begin CSEET Preparation

Your CS journey formally begins after you have passed Class 12 from a recognized board — any stream is acceptable, though a Commerce background is a genuine advantage. As soon as you have your Class 12 results, register for the CSEET on ICSI's official website (www.icsi.edu) and begin your preparation. The CSEET covers Business Communication, Legal Aptitude, Logical Reasoning, Economic and Business Environment, and Current Affairs — a broad range of topics that require a systematic study approach. Prepare with ICSI's official study materials, which are comprehensive and specifically calibrated to the examination's requirements. Develop the habit of reading quality newspapers and business publications every day — current affairs is both a specific examination subject and a general professional necessity for a career in corporate governance.

Step 2: Pass the CSEET and Register for the Executive Programme

Approach the CSEET with the seriousness it deserves — not as a hurdle to be cleared minimally, but as the first demonstration of the professional commitment and intellectual capability that your CS career will require. After passing the CSEET, register for the CS Executive Programme promptly. Study with the goal of genuine understanding rather than examination performance alone — the Executive Programme curriculum covers the substantive knowledge base that you will draw on throughout your professional career, and shallow preparation now will create professional gaps that compound over time. The modules covered in the Executive Programme — Company Law, Financial and Strategic Management, Tax Laws, Economic, Business and Commercial Laws, and Corporate Governance — are the intellectual foundations of everything you will do as a CS professional.

Step 3: Complete Executive Programme Training Requirements

After passing the Executive Programme examinations, do not treat the training requirements — the one-month Executive Development Programme and the 21 months of practical training — as bureaucratic boxes to be ticked. Treat them as the genuinely valuable professional development opportunities they are designed to be. The practical training in particular is an irreplaceable educational experience — a chance to observe and participate in real corporate governance work, to understand how the principles you have studied in textbooks translate into actual organizational practice, and to build the professional relationships and professional reputation that will underpin your early career. Choose your training organization thoughtfully — look for companies or practicing CS firms that will give you genuine exposure to a wide range of CS work, not just administrative tasks that do not build real professional capability.

Step 4: Complete the Professional Programme and Earn Your CS Designation

After completing the Executive Programme and its associated training requirements, register for the Professional Programme and approach it with the depth and commitment it demands. The Professional Programme examinations are the most advanced assessments in the CS qualification journey, covering topics including Advanced Company Law and Practice, Secretarial Audit and Due Diligence, Corporate Restructuring, Insolvency Law, Resolution of Corporate Disputes, Securities Laws and Capital Markets, and Economic Laws. This is the level at which you develop the expertise that will define you as a senior CS professional — and the depth of preparation you bring to it will directly shape the quality of your professional practice in the years ahead. Passing the Professional Programme examinations and completing all required training earns you the CS designation and ICSI membership — the qualification that opens every door in the corporate governance profession.

Step 5: Pursue Continuous Professional Development

The CS qualification is not a destination — it is a foundation. Corporate law evolves. Regulatory frameworks change. New governance challenges emerge. The Company Secretary who earned their qualification five years ago and has not updated their knowledge since then is a Company Secretary who is no longer fully equipped to serve their clients and employers effectively. ICSI requires its members to complete a specified number of Continuing Professional Education (CPE) hours each year — but the best Company Secretaries go far beyond this minimum requirement, engaging with the profession's evolving body of knowledge through conferences, seminars, professional publications, and their own sustained intellectual engagement with the legal and regulatory developments that affect their practice.

Pro Tip for CS Aspirants:

Visit ICSI's official website (www.icsi.edu) today and download the CSEET study materials — they are available free of charge to registered candidates and represent the most authoritative and directly relevant preparation resources available. Read the Companies Act, 2013 — not just study notes about it, but the actual legislation. Develop the habit of reading The Economic Times and Business Standard every day. The CS profession demands professionals who are genuinely engaged with the business and regulatory world they serve — and building that engagement as a habit, from the earliest days of your preparation, will give you an advantage that no amount of last-minute examination cramming can replicate. The best Company Secretaries are those who are genuinely curious about the corporate world. Cultivate that curiosity now.

Career Opportunities After the CS Course

One of the most genuinely exciting aspects of the CS qualification is the breadth and variety of the career opportunities it opens. The CS designation is not a narrow credential that qualifies you for one type of role in one type of organization. It is a multi-dimensional professional qualification that is valued across a remarkable range of corporate functions and organizational contexts.

The most direct application of the CS qualification is in the areas of Legal Secretarial Work and Corporate Governance — serving as the Secretary to the Board, managing regulatory compliance, and ensuring that the company's legal obligations are met completely and on time. But the CS's knowledge base extends far beyond this core function into Foreign Collaboration and Joint Ventures (advising on the legal and regulatory requirements of international business partnerships), Corporate Restructuring (guiding mergers, acquisitions, demergers, and insolvency proceedings), Financial Management (contributing to treasury, capital structure, and investment decision-making), Arbitration and Conciliation (representing companies in alternative dispute resolution proceedings), Project Planning, Due Diligence, Capital Markets and Investor Relations, and Corporate Advisory Services across the full range of issues that arise in a complex corporate organization.

In terms of specific job positions, qualified CS professionals can pursue roles including Assistant to the Board of Directors, Company Registrar, Legal Advisor, Corporate Policy Maker, Chief Administrative Officer, Principal Secretary, Corporate Planner, Chairman and Managing Director's Office, Administrative Assistant, Administrative Secretary, and Investor and Capital Market Relations Manager. The range of these roles — from operational and administrative to strategic and advisory — reflects the genuine versatility of the CS qualification and its value across the full organizational hierarchy from executive support to C-suite advisory.

Future Scope — The Decade Ahead for Company Secretaries

Is there any credible argument that the demand for Company Secretaries in India will diminish over the coming decade? Examined honestly, the evidence points firmly in the opposite direction. India's corporate sector is growing. The number of companies crossing the paid-up share capital threshold that triggers the mandatory CS appointment requirement is increasing every year. Regulatory frameworks are becoming more complex, not simpler — with new obligations around environmental, social, and governance (ESG) reporting, data protection compliance, and international regulatory alignment being layered onto an already demanding compliance environment. And the consequences of governance failures — both for companies and for their directors personally — are becoming more severe as enforcement agencies become more active and shareholder expectations rise.

All of these trends point toward more demand for CS professionals, not less. And the nature of that demand is also evolving in ways that are highly favorable for well-prepared CS candidates. The traditional view of the CS as primarily a compliance function — important but largely operational — is giving way to a broader understanding of the CS as a strategic governance advisor who contributes actively to the highest-level discussions about how the company should be governed, how it should manage its legal risks, and how it should present itself to regulators, investors, and the public. This elevation of the CS role from operational to strategic is one of the most exciting developments in the profession, and it creates enormous opportunities for CS professionals who have the intellectual breadth, the business acumen, and the communication skills to seize it.

The intersection of technology and corporate governance is creating additional new opportunities. Digital board management platforms, automated compliance monitoring systems, electronic shareholder communications, and the digitization of regulatory filing processes are all changing the operational landscape of CS practice — and CS professionals who embrace these technologies and develop genuine proficiency with them will be significantly more effective, and significantly more valuable, than those who resist the digital transformation of their profession.

Conclusion: The Boardroom Is Waiting for You

There is a boardroom in India — many boardrooms, in companies large and small, across every sector of the economy — where a vacant chair is waiting. Not a metaphorical vacancy, but a real one: a chair that belongs, by law and by organizational need, to a qualified Company Secretary. A professional who combines deep knowledge of corporate law with the communication skills to translate that knowledge into clear, actionable guidance. A professional who can sit beside the Chairman and the Managing Director and speak with the authority that comes from genuine expertise and genuine preparation. A professional who understands that their role is not just to keep the company compliant — it is to help the company be good. To be well-governed. To be trustworthy. To be the kind of organization that its shareholders, its employees, its customers, and its regulators can have genuine confidence in.

The path to occupying that chair — the CSEET, the Executive Programme, the Professional Programme, the training, the years of building expertise and professional reputation — is demanding. It will require of you a sustained commitment that extends well beyond any single examination. It will test your patience, your discipline, and your intellectual endurance.
