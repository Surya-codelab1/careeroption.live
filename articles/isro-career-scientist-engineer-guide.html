Complete ISRO Career Guide 2026 — How to Get a Job at India's Greatest Space Agency

By Admin

Introduction: The Institution That Watches Over Every Indian

Consider for a moment how much of your daily life depends on something you can neither see nor touch — something orbiting silently hundreds of kilometres above your head, watching, measuring, calculating, and transmitting information to scientists and engineers who use it to protect the lives of millions of people every single day. When a cyclone forms over the Bay of Bengal, it is satellite data that gives meteorologists enough warning to evacuate coastal communities before the storm makes landfall. When floodwaters begin to rise in a river basin, it is satellite imagery that tells disaster management teams exactly where the danger is most acute and which villages need immediate rescue. When a drought begins to develop in a farming district, it is remote sensing data from space that gives agricultural planners early warning that food security is at risk.

Before India had this capability, natural disasters arrived without warning. Cyclones killed hundreds of thousands of people who had no time to flee. Floods wiped out entire communities before any help could be organised. The suffering was immense, the losses incalculable, and the helplessness of governments and communities in the face of nature's indifference was heartbreaking. The transformation from that era of helplessness to this era of forewarning and preparation has been achieved, in large part, through the extraordinary work of one institution: the Indian Space Research Organisation — ISRO.

Every country that takes its responsibilities to its citizens seriously maintains a research organisation dedicated to understanding the Earth and the cosmos through the instruments of space technology. India's organisation — ISRO — is not merely competent. It is, by any objective measure, one of the finest space agencies in the world, and one of the most extraordinary institutions that any scientist or engineer in India could hope to join. If you have ever looked up at the night sky and felt the pull of something vast and unknowable, if science fills you not just with knowledge but with genuine wonder and ambition, and if you want your career to mean something far larger than a salary and a job title, then this guide was written for you.

What Is ISRO? India's National Space Institution

ISRO stands for the Indian Space Research Organisation — in Hindi, Bharatiya Antariksh Anusandhan Sangathan. It is India's national space agency, established on August 15, 1969 — a date chosen with unmistakable symbolism, India's Independence Day — and its headquarters are located in Bengaluru, Karnataka, the city that has become the undisputed capital of India's science and technology excellence.

ISRO is administered by the Department of Space, which reports directly to the Prime Minister of India. This reporting structure is not a bureaucratic technicality. It reflects the extraordinary strategic importance that India's government places on its space programme — the recognition, at the very highest level of national leadership, that space technology is not a luxury or an indulgence but a foundational national capability on which India's security, its economic development, and its scientific standing in the world all depend.

The institution was established largely through the vision, energy, and determination of one extraordinary man: Dr. Vikram Ambalal Sarabhai, the physicist and industrialist who is rightly recognised as the Father of the Indian Space Programme. Dr. Sarabhai possessed a quality that is rare among scientists and rarer still among administrators — the ability to see, with complete clarity, what a nation needed not just in the present but decades into the future. He understood, in the early 1960s, when India was still finding its footing as a newly independent nation, that space technology would become indispensable to national development, and he devoted his remarkable intellect and boundless energy to building the institutional foundations that made ISRO possible. Every satellite that ISRO has launched, every mission that has carried India's name into the cosmos, every life that has been saved by early warning systems built on ISRO's data — all of it traces its origins to the vision that Vikram Sarabhai planted in those early years.

"To work at ISRO is to work on problems that matter to every Indian — not abstractly, not philosophically, but concretely and immediately. It is to be part of the institution that watches over the subcontinent from space and uses what it sees to keep people safe, informed, and connected."

ISRO's Extraordinary Achievements: Why This Organisation Is Truly World-Class

To fully appreciate what it means to join ISRO, you must first truly understand what ISRO has achieved — because the accomplishments of this institution are not just impressive by the standards of a developing nation. They are impressive by the standards of the entire world, and they have been achieved at a fraction of the cost that any comparable programme in Europe or America has required.

ISRO is ranked among the top five space agencies in the world — a standing that places it in the company of NASA, ESA, Roscosmos, and JAXA. It has launched more than 370 satellites in total, of which 101 have served India's own national needs and 269 have been launched on behalf of foreign countries — making ISRO a trusted and commercially competitive satellite launch provider for the global space industry. This commercial success is not just financially significant. It is a statement of technical excellence that the most advanced nations on Earth have validated by paying ISRO to carry their satellites into orbit.

India's first satellite, Aryabhata — named in honour of the great ancient Indian mathematician and astronomer — was launched on April 19, 1975. It was a modest beginning in technical terms, but a moment of profound national pride and symbolic significance, a declaration that independent India had joined the community of spacefaring nations. Five years later, on July 18, 1980, the satellite Rohini became the first satellite placed in orbit by an Indian-made launch vehicle — the SLV-3 — marking the moment when India became not just a user of space but a builder of the means to reach it.

Since those pioneering achievements, ISRO has built and deployed the Indian National Satellite System — INSAT — which has revolutionised telecommunications, television broadcasting, meteorological observation, and disaster warning across the Indian subcontinent. It has built the Indian Remote Sensing — IRS — satellite series, which provides the detailed Earth observation data that supports agriculture, forestry, urban planning, and water resource management. The Chandrayaan missions have carried India to the Moon and, in Chandrayaan-3's historic soft landing on the lunar south pole in 2023, made India the fourth country in history to achieve a soft lunar landing — and the first in the world to land near the Moon's south pole, a scientifically invaluable region that no other nation had reached before. The Mars Orbiter Mission — Mangalyaan — placed India in orbit around Mars on its very first attempt, making India the first Asian nation to reach Mars and the only country in the world to do so successfully on a debut interplanetary mission.

These achievements did not happen by accident. They happened because generations of brilliant, passionate, deeply committed Indian scientists and engineers chose to give their careers to ISRO — to work on problems that mattered, in an institution that valued excellence, for a mission that served their nation and advanced the cause of human knowledge. You could be the next person to make that choice.

ISRO's Mission: What the Organisation Is Working Toward

Understanding ISRO's current and future mission is essential before you decide to pursue a career there, because the mission defines the work — and the work is what you will spend your professional life doing.

ISRO's core mandate is to develop space technology and apply it to India's national development. This mandate encompasses an extraordinary range of activities: designing and launching communication satellites that bring connectivity to remote parts of India where terrestrial infrastructure cannot reach; operating Earth observation satellites that monitor agriculture, disasters, forests, coastlines, and urban growth; developing the launch vehicles — the rockets — that carry Indian and foreign satellites into orbit; building the navigation satellite systems that make India's digital economy and its defence capabilities more reliable and precise; and conducting deep-space exploration missions that advance humanity's understanding of the solar system and the universe beyond.

Looking forward, ISRO's agenda for the coming decade is among the most ambitious in its history. The Gaganyaan mission will carry Indian astronauts into orbit for the first time — making India only the fourth country in history to achieve human spaceflight with its own technology. The Chandrayaan and Mangalyaan successor missions will push India's exploration of the Moon and Mars to new depths of scientific understanding. ISRO is actively planning missions to Venus and to the outer solar system. And the development of next-generation heavy-lift launch vehicles will dramatically expand India's capacity to place large payloads into orbit, opening new possibilities for both national programmes and commercial services.

To pursue all of this, ISRO needs brilliant, dedicated people — engineers, scientists, mathematicians, computer scientists, and specialists of many kinds. It needs them not just in large numbers but at the highest levels of quality. And it recruits them, carefully and competitively, from among the best that India's educational system produces.

Who Can Apply for ISRO Jobs? All Eligible Categories

One of the most important things to understand about ISRO's recruitment is that the organisation does not hire only one type of person. While scientists and engineers form the core of ISRO's technical workforce, the institution employs a wide range of professionals, and candidates at multiple educational levels are eligible to apply.

Graduate, postgraduate, engineering, science, and technical diploma holders can all apply for various positions at ISRO, depending on their specific qualifications and the requirements of particular roles. Young graduates are most commonly recruited as Scientists or Engineers — and this is, for most aspirants, the most desirable and most sought-after pathway into the organisation. These scientific and engineering roles are filled from among candidates who have qualified in fields including Electronics Engineering, Mechanical Engineering, Electrical Engineering, Civil Engineering, and Computer Science — the technical disciplines that are most directly relevant to the design, construction, and operation of rockets and spacecraft.

It is worth understanding that ISRO does not recruit continuously throughout the year. Recruitment happens periodically, as vacancies arise and as the organisation's evolving needs require new talent. This means that the aspiring ISRO candidate must be patient, must stay informed about recruitment notifications through ISRO's official channels, and must be ready to apply promptly and competitively when the opportunity presents itself.

Eligibility and Qualifications: What You Need to Join ISRO

Let us be completely precise about the academic requirements, because precision is what ISRO values and precision is what this guide owes you.

Core Academic Requirements for Scientist/Engineer Roles:

To apply for Scientist or Engineer positions at ISRO, you must hold a degree in one of the following: B.E. or B.Tech (Bachelor of Engineering or Technology), B.Sc Engineering, a 3-year Diploma in Engineering, M.Sc, or an equivalent qualification. The degree must be in a relevant technical discipline — Civil, Electrical, Mechanical, Electronics, or Computer Science Engineering. A minimum of 65% aggregate marks in your qualifying degree is required. This is a firm threshold, not a suggestion.

The educational disciplines that are most directly relevant to ISRO's work, and from which the largest numbers of Scientists and Engineers are recruited, are:

  • Electronics Engineering — The backbone of satellite design, communication systems, onboard instrumentation, and ground station technology
  • Mechanical Engineering — Essential for launch vehicle design, propulsion systems, spacecraft structural engineering, and thermal management
  • Electrical Engineering — Critical for power systems, control systems, and the electrical architecture of spacecraft and launch vehicles
  • Computer Science Engineering — Increasingly central to ISRO's work as software systems, data processing, autonomous navigation, and AI-based analysis become more important across all mission types
  • Civil Engineering — Required for the design, construction, and maintenance of ISRO's extensive ground infrastructure — launch pads, tracking stations, test facilities, and administrative campuses

Age Eligibility

For General Category candidates, the upper age limit for ISRO recruitment is 35 years. For candidates belonging to Scheduled Caste (SC) and Scheduled Tribe (ST) categories, a relaxation of 5 years is provided, making their upper age limit 40 years. Other Backward Class (OBC) candidates receive a relaxation of 3 years, with an upper age limit of 38 years. These age limits apply at the time of application and are strictly observed.

Pathways to ISRO: The Routes You Can Take

There are several distinct pathways through which a qualified candidate can enter ISRO. Understanding each of them clearly allows you to plan your preparation with precision and purpose.

Pathway 1: ISRO Centralised Recruitment Board (ICRB) Examination

The most common and most widely used pathway into ISRO for engineering graduates is through the ISRO Centralised Recruitment Board — the ICRB. This is a written examination conducted by ISRO specifically to identify and recruit qualified candidates from engineering and science backgrounds who have completed their education at institutions other than IIST. The ICRB examination tests candidates on their technical knowledge in their chosen engineering discipline, as well as on general aptitude and analytical reasoning.

Candidates who clear the written examination are shortlisted for a personal interview, at which ISRO's selection panel assesses not just technical knowledge but analytical thinking, problem-solving ability, communication skills, and the kind of intellectual curiosity and genuine passion for space science that ISRO values in its people. Success in both the written examination and the interview leads to appointment as a Scientist or Engineer at ISRO.

Notifications for the ICRB examination are published on ISRO's official website. It is absolutely essential that you monitor this website regularly and respond to notifications promptly, as application windows are typically open for only a limited period.

Pathway 2: Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST), Thiruvananthapuram

The most direct and prestigious pathway into ISRO is through the Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology — IIST — located in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala. IIST is a deemed university established by the Department of Space specifically to train the next generation of space scientists and engineers for ISRO and the broader national space programme. It is, in the most literal sense, a pipeline from classroom to spacecraft — an institution whose entire educational mission is oriented toward producing the kind of talent that ISRO needs.

Admission to IIST's undergraduate programmes is through the JEE Advanced examination — the same highly competitive national entrance test used for admission to the IITs. A strong JEE Advanced rank is required, and within IIST's degree programmes, students must maintain a minimum of 75% marks in their coursework to remain eligible for direct absorption into ISRO after graduation. For those who meet this academic standard, IIST graduates are offered positions at ISRO without the need to go through the competitive ICRB examination — a direct, guaranteed pathway that makes IIST one of the most strategically valuable educational destinations for any aspiring ISRO professional.

Pathway 3: IIT, NIT, and Other Premier Institution Graduates

Graduates who have completed their engineering or science degrees from the Indian Institutes of Technology, National Institutes of Technology, or other recognised institutions of national standing are also eligible to apply for ISRO positions through the ICRB examination. The strength of an IIT or NIT degree — its rigorous curriculum, its culture of research and problem-solving, and its signal of academic excellence — is well recognised by ISRO, and candidates from these institutions are typically very competitive in the recruitment process.

A Strategic Truth for ISRO Aspirants:

The single most powerful thing you can do to improve your chances of joining ISRO is to develop genuine, deep technical mastery in your chosen engineering discipline — not surface-level familiarity sufficient to pass examinations, but the kind of understanding that allows you to apply your knowledge creatively to problems you have never seen before. ISRO selects for intellectual depth, not just academic credentials. The candidates who succeed are those who have genuinely internalised the principles of their field and can demonstrate that understanding under pressure.

The ISRO Recruitment Process: Step by Step

Understanding the recruitment process in detail allows you to prepare for each stage with appropriate focus and to approach the process with the confidence that comes from genuine preparation.

  1. Watch for Official Notifications — ISRO publishes all recruitment notifications on its official website. Monitor this resource regularly. When a notification is published that matches your qualifications and interests, read it with extreme care — every detail about eligibility, required qualifications, application procedures, and deadlines matters.
  2. Submit Your Application — Navigate to the official ISRO website, locate the Careers section, and complete the online application form for the position you are targeting. Fill every field accurately and completely. Submit the form before the deadline and retain a copy of your submission confirmation for your records.
  3. Shortlisting — ISRO's recruitment team reviews all applications against the stated eligibility criteria and academic requirements. Candidates who meet the requirements and whose qualifications are strongest are shortlisted to proceed to the written examination. Competition at this stage is intense, and having academic credentials that exceed the minimum requirements significantly improves your chances of being shortlisted.
  4. Written Examination (ICRB) — Shortlisted candidates are called to sit the written examination conducted by the ISRO Centralised Recruitment Board. This examination tests technical knowledge in your engineering or science discipline, general aptitude, and analytical reasoning. Prepare for this examination with the same rigour and discipline that you brought to your engineering degree — because the candidates you are competing against are among the most academically capable young professionals in India.
  5. Personal Interview — Candidates who successfully clear the written examination are called for a personal interview with ISRO's selection panel. The interview assesses your technical depth, your problem-solving approach, your awareness of ISRO's current programmes and future plans, your communication skills, and — perhaps most importantly — the authenticity and depth of your passion for space science and engineering. Be prepared to discuss your academic projects, your understanding of ISRO's missions, and the specific ways in which your skills and knowledge can contribute to the organisation's goals.
  6. Final Selection and Appointment — Candidates who perform successfully in both the written examination and the interview are offered appointment as Scientists or Engineers at ISRO. You will be assigned to one of ISRO's numerous centres across India based on your specialisation and the organisation's current staffing needs.

The Full Value of an ISRO Package — Beyond the Salary:

The financial compensation at ISRO must be understood in the context of the comprehensive benefits package that accompanies it. ISRO employees receive subsidised or free housing in well-maintained residential colonies adjacent to their work centres. Free or heavily subsidised transportation between residential colonies and work facilities is provided. Comprehensive medical coverage is extended to the employee and their entire immediate family, covering all major medical expenses at ISRO's own medical facilities and at empanelled hospitals. Children's education allowances, generous leave provisions, and access to recreational and cultural facilities at ISRO campuses further enhance the quality of life that ISRO employment provides. The total value of this package, including benefits, is substantially higher than the salary figure alone suggests.

ISRO's Major Centres Across India

ISRO is not a single facility but a network of specialised centres distributed across the country, each focused on a different aspect of the space programme. Understanding these centres helps you appreciate the breadth of ISRO's work and the variety of roles available within it.

  • ISRO Headquarters, Bengaluru — The nerve centre of the entire organisation, housing the leadership, strategic planning, and overall programme management functions
  • Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre (VSSC), Thiruvananthapuram — The principal launch vehicle development centre, where the rockets that carry India's satellites into space are designed, tested, and built
  • ISRO Satellite Centre (ISAC) / U R Rao Satellite Centre, Bengaluru — The primary satellite design and development facility, where India's communication, Earth observation, and navigation satellites are built
  • Satish Dhawan Space Centre (SDSC-SHAR), Sriharikota — ISRO's launch port, located on an island off the coast of Andhra Pradesh, from which all Indian orbital launches take place
  • Space Applications Centre (SAC), Ahmedabad — Focused on developing the applications of space technology for national development — meteorology, remote sensing, navigation, and communication
  • National Remote Sensing Centre (NRSC), Hyderabad — India's centre for satellite-based Earth observation, managing the acquisition, processing, and distribution of remote sensing data for agriculture, forestry, disaster management, and urban planning
  • Liquid Propulsion Systems Centre (LPSC), Thiruvananthapuram and Bengaluru — Developing the liquid fuel propulsion systems that power India's launch vehicles and spacecraft manoeuvring systems

Step-by-Step Career Roadmap to Joining ISRO

Phase 1: Build Your Academic Foundation (Class 9–12)

  1. Develop genuine mastery in Physics and Mathematics — not just sufficient understanding to pass examinations, but deep, intuitive fluency. These subjects are the language in which space science and engineering are spoken, and you must be able to think in them naturally and confidently.
  2. Add Chemistry to your core study disciplines — it is mandatory for JEE eligibility and directly relevant to propulsion chemistry, materials science, and the design of spacecraft systems that must function in extreme environments.
  3. Develop an active, engaged interest in India's space programme. Follow ISRO's missions. Read about satellite technology, rocket propulsion, and orbital mechanics at a level beyond your school curriculum. Let your curiosity about space become a genuine, self-directed intellectual pursuit rather than just a subject on an examination syllabus.
  4. Prepare rigorously for JEE — both Mains and Advanced. Your JEE score determines which institutions you can access, and the quality of your engineering education significantly influences your competitiveness for ISRO recruitment. Treat JEE preparation as the serious, long-term commitment it is.

Phase 2: Engineering Degree (4 Years)

  1. Choose your engineering discipline thoughtfully. Electronics, Mechanical, Electrical, Computer Science, and Aerospace Engineering are all highly relevant to ISRO's work. Choose the one that genuinely excites you most — because sustained excellence requires genuine passion, and ISRO demands excellence.
  2. If you gain admission to IIST through JEE Advanced, commit to maintaining the 75% academic performance threshold required for direct ISRO absorption. This is your most direct possible route into the organisation, and it is worth every effort to protect it.
  3. If you are studying at an IIT, NIT, or other strong institution, target the ISRO ICRB examination as your primary post-graduation pathway. Begin familiarising yourself with the examination's structure and content from your second year onward.
  4. Pursue research projects, internships, and academic collaborations that are directly relevant to space applications — satellite design, control systems, propulsion, remote sensing, navigation. These experiences build genuine competence and demonstrate to ISRO's selectors that your interest in space science is real and substantive.
  5. Maintain academic performance above 65% aggregate — the minimum required for ISRO eligibility — and target 75% or above, because stronger academic performance makes you more competitive at every stage of ISRO's recruitment process.

Phase 3: Recruitment and Early Career

  1. Monitor ISRO's official recruitment notifications consistently after graduation. Set up notifications and check regularly so that you never miss an application window.
  2. Prepare specifically and intensively for the ICRB written examination. Study your core engineering subjects to examination standard, practise past papers if they are available, and develop your analytical reasoning skills alongside your technical knowledge.
  3. Prepare for the personal interview with genuine depth. Know ISRO's current missions, recent achievements, and future plans in detail. Be ready to discuss your own projects and how they are relevant to ISRO's work. Demonstrate passion that is real, knowledge that is deep, and the intellectual honesty to acknowledge the limits of what you know.
  4. If you are not selected on your first attempt, do not be discouraged. Competition for ISRO positions is extraordinarily intense, and many of ISRO's most successful scientists and engineers were not selected on their first application. Use each attempt as a diagnostic — to identify gaps in your knowledge, to sharpen your examination performance, and to deepen your understanding of what ISRO is looking for.

The Skills and Qualities ISRO Looks for in Its People

Academic qualifications are the entry requirement. What ISRO truly needs — and what its selection process is designed to identify — goes considerably deeper than marks and degrees.

  • Technical Depth in Core Engineering Disciplines — Not surface familiarity but genuine, thorough understanding of the principles that underpin your chosen field — the kind of understanding that allows you to apply those principles to novel problems and to explain them clearly to others
  • Analytical and Problem-Solving Ability — Space missions involve engineering problems of extraordinary complexity and consequence. ISRO needs people who can approach problems systematically, think through multiple solution approaches, evaluate trade-offs, and arrive at robust, reliable answers under pressure
  • Precision and Attention to Detail — In space engineering, small errors have catastrophic consequences. The culture of ISRO demands a standard of precision and thoroughness that exceeds what most work environments require
  • Teamwork and Collaboration — No single engineer or scientist builds a rocket or a satellite alone. ISRO's missions are the product of thousands of people working in carefully coordinated teams across multiple specialisations and centres. The ability to work effectively within complex, multi-disciplinary teams is essential
  • Curiosity and Intellectual Ambition — ISRO's work is always at the frontier of what India's technology can do. The organisation needs people who are not content to simply apply existing knowledge but are genuinely driven to push the boundaries of what is possible
  • Patriotic Commitment — This is not a cliché. ISRO's work is national work — it serves India's security, its development, and its scientific standing in the world. The scientists and engineers who give their best to ISRO do so partly because they feel a genuine, deep connection to that national mission

Future Scope: Why an ISRO Career in 2026 Is More Exciting Than Ever

The coming decade is shaping up to be the most ambitious and consequential in ISRO's history, and that means that the scientists and engineers who join ISRO now will be working on some of the most extraordinary missions any Indian space professional has ever had the privilege of being part of.

The Gaganyaan mission — India's first crewed spaceflight — will carry Indian astronauts into Earth orbit, making India only the fourth country in history to achieve human spaceflight with its own technology. The engineers and scientists at ISRO who are working on Gaganyaan are solving problems that have never been solved before in India: life support systems, crew escape systems, human-rated launch vehicles, reentry capsule design, and the medical and operational protocols that will keep Indian astronauts alive and safe in space. To work on Gaganyaan is to work on something that will change India forever.

The expansion of India's commercial launch services — with ISRO's new generation of heavy-lift vehicles making India more competitive in the global satellite launch market — will create new engineering challenges and new opportunities. India's planned Venus orbiter mission will carry the country's scientific instruments to the cloud-shrouded planet for the first time. Future Chandrayaan missions will deepen India's exploration of the Moon. And the long-term vision of human missions to Mars — a goal that India's space programme is steadily building the capability to realise — will require the kind of sustained, multigenerational engineering effort that makes space science the most thrilling and most meaningful technical career on Earth.

India's space economy, which currently stands at around USD 8 billion, is projected to grow to USD 44 billion by 2033. The private space sector — newly opened to Indian companies through policy reforms — is beginning to complement ISRO's capabilities with an ecosystem of startups and established companies building satellites, launch vehicles, propulsion systems, and space applications. An ISRO career in 2026 does not just mean contributing to a great institution. It means being at the centre of a transformation that will define India's place in the cosmos for the next century.

Conclusion: Your Nation Is Calling — Will You Answer?

There is a particular quality to the ambition of a young Indian who wants to work at ISRO that is different from any other career aspiration — and it is worth naming honestly. It is not just personal ambition, the desire for a prestigious title and a good salary, though those things are real and legitimate. It is something larger. It is the desire to be part of something that matters — not to you alone but to hundreds of millions of people who depend, every time a cyclone warning saves their lives or a satellite call connects them to a loved one far away, on the invisible infrastructure that ISRO has built and maintains in space on their behalf.

That desire — to do work that matters at the scale of a nation, to contribute your intelligence and your dedication to problems that are genuinely important — is the most powerful and most honourable form of ambition there is. And ISRO, more than almost any other institution in India, offers the opportunity to live that ambition out in full.

The road is demanding. The competition is fierce. The academic requirements are real, and meeting them requires years of sustained, disciplined effort. But every ISRO scientist and engineer who has walked through those gates and shown their badge for the first time will tell you that the moment they did, they understood that every hour of study, every examination, every setback and recovery along the way had been worth it completely and without reservation.

Your nation built rockets from scratch. It sent a mission to Mars on its first attempt. It landed on the south pole of the Moon before any other country in the world. It did all of this not with unlimited resources or inherited technology but with the intelligence, the dedication, and the unbreakable ambition of its own people. Those people started exactly where you are — as students, as dreamers, as young Indians with science in their heads and stars in their eyes. They studied. They prepared. They persisted. And they built something that the whole world now admires. You could be next. Begin today.
